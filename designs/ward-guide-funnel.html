Priming, not prescribing: “nothing to fill in now,” skills are optional taps, and “skip” is one press. It lowers cold-start friction on an unfamiliar ward without telling the student what their placement will contain — dodging the scope-of-practice liability.

Why this version survives the stress test. The guide trades in experience and prompts, never facts or protocols — so it can’t go out of date or be wrong per facility, and it doesn’t put your clinical credibility on the line for content you can’t maintain across eight wards. It’s ungated, so it builds goodwill instead of resentment. The conversion sits after the value, framed as reciprocity. And it’s wired to the capture loop — likely skills carry standard tags, prompts match the app’s voice — so the funnel’s payoff is the product itself, not a dead-end PDF.

The honest ceiling. Authored guides are still founder content with a maintenance cost — each new ward is writing you have to do and keep fresh. The version with no ceiling is the one generated from the corpus (“students on cardiac wards most often log these skills”), which is impossible until you have students and data. Authored guides are the bridge to that, not the destination — and writing ten of them before the core loop is proven to retain is the same trap we’ve flagged each round. One guide, for the most common first placement, is the test. Not a library.
